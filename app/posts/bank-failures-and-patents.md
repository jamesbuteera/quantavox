Banks serve a monumental purpose — ranging from the custody of deposits to the strategic lending of those funds to third parties. They operate like a hidden motor, rarely seen or celebrated, yet their impact is felt in every facet of human endeavor.

When banks are successful, the economy flourishes, and society often takes their stability for granted. However, when a bank's doors close permanently, panic ensues. From billionaires to the impoverished, and from government officials to faith leaders, the collapse of a financial institution causes a fundamental crisis of confidence.

## The research question

The goal of my long-term research is to quantify this impact: when banks fail, do patent filings increase or decrease? By analyzing these trends across local, small, and large-scale banks, I aim to determine if the success of American innovation is intrinsically tied to the health of our banking institutions.

## Silicon Valley Bank as a case study

The collapse of Silicon Valley Bank sent shockwaves not only through the financial sector but also through the world's most active entrepreneurial hubs. SVB was not just a bank — it was the financial backbone of the startup ecosystem. When it failed in March 2023, thousands of companies suddenly faced uncertainty about their deposits, payroll, and future funding.

This event serves as a modern case study for my research. I am utilizing Andrew Metrick's analysis from the Journal of Economic Perspectives to understand the contemporary context of bank failures and their subsequent effect on the patent landscape.

## Methodology

My approach involves building a comprehensive Python-based dataset that links FDIC bank failure records with USPTO patent filing data across multiple time periods and geographic regions.

Key variables include:
- Bank failure dates and asset sizes
- Patent filing counts by technology class
- Regional economic indicators
- Venture capital activity as a control variable

## What the data is telling us so far

Early analysis suggests that the relationship between bank failures and patent activity is not linear. Large bank failures appear to temporarily suppress patent filings in affected regions, while small community bank failures show a more complex pattern — potentially accelerating innovation as entrepreneurs seek alternative funding mechanisms.

This research is ongoing. Follow along as the dataset grows and the picture becomes clearer.
